UX Team Skills Assessment Tool

From Basic Survey to Strategic Team Optimization: Building a Skills Assessment System

Project Overview

Nobody asked me to build this. When two UX teams merged, leadership ran a surface-level skills survey that produced little actionable insight. I saw an organizational problem worth solving and built the tool myself — on my own time, using techniques I'd developed for a completely different purpose.

Team assignments were essentially based on guesswork rather than data-driven understanding of individual strengths, interests, and optimal team compositions. The tool I built changed that — and my manager used it regularly to staff new projects.

The Challenge

The merged UX organization faced a critical knowledge gap. With team assignments based on intuition rather than data, leadership couldn't effectively:

  • Identify optimal team compositions for projects
  • Understand individual capabilities and growth areas
  • Make strategic hiring or training decisions
  • Leverage hidden strengths within the team

The Core Problem: A superficial skills inventory was producing data that felt authoritative but wasn't — and nobody had built the infrastructure to make it actually useful. I decided to fix that.

The raw data underneath — 2,500+ rows of participant/skill combinations, each rated across expertise, interest, and multiple role dimensions. Useful only once systematically queried.

An Unexpected Foundation

From Overwatch to UX Team Optimization

My expertise with Google Sheets' QUERY function came from an unexpected source: analyzing my own Overwatch gameplay statistics. I had built complex spreadsheets to track damage output, kill ratios, and survival metrics to identify improvement areas in my gaming performance.

When I saw the organizational problem, I recognized that the analytical structure was the same — multi-dimensional data about individual performance, sliced to find patterns and optimize composition. The domain was different. The system was identical.

Key Skills Transfer:

  • Multi-dimensional analysis: Gaming stats (damage/kills/survival) → UX skills (expertise/interest/role fit)
  • Performance optimization: Personal gameplay improvement → Team composition optimization
  • Pattern recognition: Identifying gameplay weaknesses → Discovering organizational skill gaps

Solution Architecture

Comprehensive Skills Database

I created a centralized data system capturing comprehensive skills assessment across 120 different UX competencies. Each team member rated their expertise level and interest level for every skill, creating a rich multi-dimensional dataset.

Key Design Decisions

Confronting Self-Assessment Psychology

As the tool got used, a predictable problem emerged: the data wasn't as reliable as it looked. I recognized the Dunning-Kruger effect playing out in the responses — team members with limited expertise were overrating their abilities, while highly skilled individuals who understood the depth of their knowledge gaps rated themselves more conservatively. True experts appeared less capable than novices on paper.

The Problem

Self-assessment data distortions meant the tool could actively mislead staffing decisions — exactly the opposite of what it was built to do. Someone who'd mastered a skill and knew its nuances might rate themselves lower than a junior who didn't know what they didn't know.

The Solution I Built

I designed and built a dual-assessment framework: both the employee and their manager complete the assessment independently, then a comparison view surfaces the gaps. Rather than using the data to staff projects, you'd first use it to have a calibration conversation. I shipped it — though the org didn't end up adopting the workflow, the infrastructure was there.

From Spreadsheet to App

As the system matured, I designed a companion mobile app — Team Talent — that made the data accessible to leadership without requiring any Sheets literacy. Three views covered the core use cases.

Details tab — individual profile with service and role scores, searchable and sortable skill list with proficiency level and interest indicator

Overview tab — select any skill to see the full team ranked by proficiency, with interest indicators showing who actually wants to use it

Comparison View — Design Iteration

The comparison view went through three distinct layouts before landing on the final approach. The challenge was representing two teams' skill levels against each other in a way that was scannable at a glance.

Three comparison view iterations — A (mirrored bars from center), B (single direction with clearer labeling), C (refined spacing). B and C tested better for quick scanning.

Final comparison view — team members stacked per side, skills listed with bar charts showing relative proficiency. Add Teammate+ supports multi-person team comparisons.

Sort and filter interaction — leadership could re-sort by either team's strength, making it easy to identify where one team significantly outperformed the other

What I'd Do Differently

  • Drive Adoption of the Dual Assessment: I built the manager/employee comparison view and shipped it, but the org never adopted it as a workflow. In retrospect I'd have paired the tool launch with a structured onboarding session specifically around that feature — the infrastructure was there, the behavior change wasn't.
  • Automated Data Refresh: Bi-annual manual surveys created a maintenance burden. I'd integrate with HR systems to keep data fresher without requiring a full survey cycle.
  • Calibration Exercises: Peer validation or structured calibration sessions at the start of each assessment cycle would reduce Dunning-Kruger distortion at the source rather than correcting for it downstream.
