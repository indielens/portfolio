Curious by nature.
Rigorous by practice.

I'm Grant Thomas — a UX Designer and Product Strategist based in Brooklyn, NY. For over a decade I've been turning complicated systems into experiences that actually make sense to the humans using them.

My path to UX design started in an unexpected place: exploring settings pages in video games as a kid. I was fascinated by how small interface changes could dramatically impact the experience. Growing up immersed in digital worlds taught me to recognize what works — and more importantly, what doesn't — in user interfaces.

For the past 9+ years I've been embedded in the Eli Lilly ecosystem — working across their digital products, marketing platforms, and, since December 2022, their enterprise design system. That's one of the most complex design system environments in the pharmaceutical industry, spanning global brands, accessibility requirements, and a developer-designer audience across dozens of product teams. Across my time at Lilly I've held nearly every role — researcher, designer, Scrum Master, Product Owner, lead library architect, and lead designer. That breadth isn't accidental. I've always believed you make better design decisions when you understand what surrounds them.

Some of the work I'm most proud of: helping build Lilly.com from the ground up, partnering with Wieden+Kennedy to guide Lilly's new brand color toward an accessible, competitively differentiated system, and building out a versatile HCT-based color system that now spans Lilly's global brand portfolio — Mounjaro, Zepbound, Taltz, Basaglar, and more.

My manager had a name for a certain kind of project — the ones where something had gone sideways, the scope was unclear, or the problem didn't fit neatly into existing processes. She threw those at me. Not because I was the most senior person on the team, but because I tend to be a stabilizing presence when things are complicated. I slow down before jumping to solutions. I try to understand what everyone is actually bringing to the conversation before deciding what needs to happen. I steer away from blame and toward process — if there's a gap, I'd rather build something to fill it than find someone to fault for it.

That instinct comes partly from a long-running interest in psychology and Buddhist philosophy — specifically the idea that most people in a room are trying to do good work, and the designer's job is to create the conditions where that can actually happen. I approach stakeholder conflicts the same way I approach user research: with curiosity about what's driving the behavior, not judgment about the behavior itself.

I'm passionate about accessibility and working directly with developers to ensure designs are both beautiful and feasible in production. I don't toss work "over the wall" — I stay engaged through the build, because the decisions made in implementation matter as much as the decisions made in Figma.

Outside of work, I'm a boulderer, a reader, and a traveler who's happiest somewhere unfamiliar. I have a minor in art history and a long-standing practice of street photography inspired by Vivian Maier and Henri Cartier-Bresson. This fall I'm heading to France to complete my yoga teacher training — a commitment to both the practice and the kind of intentional discomfort that tends to produce growth.
